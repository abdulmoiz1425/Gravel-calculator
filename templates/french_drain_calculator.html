How to Calculate Gravel for a French Drain

Measure the trench in feet — convert all inch measurements before using the formula

Step-by-Step Calculation Method

  1. Measure the trench length from the water collection area to the outlet.
  2. Measure the inside trench width and the planned gravel depth.
  3. Convert inches to feet. For example, 12 inches equals 1 foot, 18 inches equals 1.5 feet.
  4. Multiply length × width × depth to find cubic feet.
  5. Subtract the pipe space if your installation includes a perforated pipe. Enter the pipe diameter to have this done automatically.
  6. Divide by 27 to convert cubic feet into cubic yards.
  7. Add a 5% to 10% waste allowance for normal trench variation, spreading, and uneven ground.
  8. Convert cubic yards into tons using your supplier's material density factor.
Common Mistake: People calculating gravel for a French drain often forget the waste allowance or supplier density factor. The calculator result is an ordering estimate, not a guaranteed delivery weight.

Worked Example: 50-Foot French Drain

A trench is 50 ft long, 12 inches wide, and 18 inches deep, with a 4-inch perforated pipe.

1 Convert: 12 in = 1 ft width, 18 in = 1.5 ft depth
2 Trench volume: 50 × 1 × 1.5 = 75 ft³
3 4-inch pipe subtracted → gravel volume ≈ 70.6 ft³
4 70.6 ÷ 27 = 2.62 yd³
5 + 10% waste: 2.62 × 1.10 = 2.88 yd³ → order ~3 yd³

At 1.4 tons/yd³, this project needs approximately 4 tons of drainage gravel. Confirm the exact density with your supplier before ordering.

What Gravel Should You Use for a French Drain?

Use clean drainage stone that leaves open spaces for water to move through the trench

Your local supplier may call it #57 stone, washed crushed stone, drainage rock, or clean angular gravel. Ask the supplier which material is best for drainage in your area and request the exact tons-per-yard weight before placing the final order.

How Much Gravel Should You Order?

Start with the calculator total, then use these checks before placing the order

Measure Inside the Trench

  • Measure the inside trench dimensions, not the wider excavation area.
  • Keep all measurements in the same unit before converting.
  • A wider measurement increases the estimate significantly.

Include Pipe Displacement

  • Enter the pipe's outside diameter for a closer gravel estimate.
  • Pipe displacement is small for short drains but matters on longer runs.
  • A 4-inch pipe in a 50-foot trench displaces about 4.4 cubic feet.

Confirm Supplier Density

  • Ask for the supplier's material density or tons-per-yard factor.
  • Gravel density changes by stone type, size, and moisture content.
  • Many suppliers sell by whole yards, half yards, or full tons.

Add a Waste Buffer

  • Add a 5% to 10% buffer for normal trench variation and spreading loss.
  • Round up to the supplier's available delivery unit.
  • Check whether the delivery truck can safely access your site.

French Drain Planning Notes Before You Dig

Gravel quantity is only one part of a working French drain — plan the full drainage route before excavation

The trench also needs a safe outlet and a consistent fall toward that outlet. A perforated pipe, clean drainage stone, and suitable filter fabric can help reduce soil movement into the gravel.

Water should move away from the problem area and discharge in a safe, permitted location. Avoid directing water toward neighboring properties, foundations, walkways, or areas that may cause erosion.

⚠ Safety Note: Call 811 before digging in the United States and follow your state's notice requirements.
